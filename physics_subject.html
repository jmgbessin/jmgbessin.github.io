One of the insights of Einstein's special relativity that hits me the most is the possibility that light has a constant speed. To understand this statement, we will need to understand relative motion, a very simple concept of speed. For example, if we take an object flying towards us, naturally running away from it will slow it down and the act of running towards it will consequently speed it up. This can be subject to confusion as when we say it is slowing down, we do not mean literally, just that the object is going to take more time to reach us, and we will perceive it as having slown down. This is the same for example, with cars. If you take two cars, one of which you are seated in, that are going at the same speed next to each other in space. How do you know if you are in motion? You do not because the car next to you travelling at the same speed will seem immobile so you have nothing to compare your motion to.

This is how relative motion redifines motion. We will need to specify our statement of not literally slowing down because the notion of speed as we know it (ie. mph,k/m) has no meaning in physics. A car that is going at 60mph will have to be precised to be moving at that speed in relation to the ground, or similarly something immobile. But again, through relative motion we can redifine immobility. For example, If you are standing at a bus stop and a car passes in front of you at 60mph. Can you not say that you passed the car at 60mph in the other direction and that the car was the one being immobile? You can, which is a sole statement of Einstein's in which anyone can declare himself in movement or immobile and be equally right.

Going back to our original statement of light speed's constancy, we mean that light travels at a certain speed (300,000 meters/sec) relative to everything. You can run away from it or run towards it, but it will take as much time to reach you in both cases. This is a seemingly paradoxical but absolutely proven and groundbreaking discovery of Einstein's relativity theory.

La Lumiere a une vitesse constante

Une des decouvertes les plus frappantes de la theorie de Relativite Speciale de Einstein est le faite que la lumiere ai une vitesse constante. Pour comprendre cela, nous devons d'abord comprendre le mouvement relatif, un concept simple mais fondamental. Par exemple, si un objet se dirige vers nous, courrir dans la direction opposee le ralentira naturellement et courrir vers l'objet fera en sorte qu'il accelere; tout cela par rapport a nous. Cela peut porter a confusion car la vitesse de l'objet n'a pas reellement changer, mais nous aurons la perception d'un ralentissement Le meme example s'applique aux voitures en mouvement. Si deux voitures se deplacent dans l'espace a une vitess egale, vous le passager n'aurais aucun moyen de savoir que vous etes en mouvement car il n'y a pas de 3eme objet relatif aux voitures. Tout mouvement peut etre decrit et percu d'une maniere relative

Cela attribut un nouveau sens a la notion du mouvement. La vitesse n'est plus une verite concrete aux yeux de la physique a cet egard. Une voiture se deplacant a 60km/hr doit avoir un mouvement definie par rapport au sol ou n'importe quel autre objet immobile. Grace a la relativite nous pouvons aussi redefinir l'immobilte. Par exemple, si vous etes assez a un arret de bus et voyez une voiture passez devant vous a une vitess de 60km/hr. Pourquoi est-ce la voiture qui se deplace dans un sens et vous immobile plutot que la voiture soit immobile et que vous vous deplaciez dans le sens contraire. D'apres la relativite d'Einstein les deux assomptions sont corrects ou neanmois egales.

Over the centuries, many debates have occured over the manifestation and movement of light. The main debate having to be settled over contradictory experiments describing light as tiny quantus of energy (particles) and as a wave, much like you might have heard of light frequency or waves. An experiment that showed light's particle like behavior was the photoelectric effect experiment. This experiment was carried out by "throwing" light (photons) at metal constituents. Scientists observed that electrons were shown to be ejected from the metal constituent, an enigma only possibly explained by light having small packets of energy, then called light quantas.

To more closely examine this effect, we zoom down to the (almost) most basic constituent of matter: the atom. This atom is comprised of three basic particles, two of which are called protons and electrons. One analogy that can help visualize an atom is similar to that of the solar system with a sun and multiple planets orbiting this sun. The atom can be imagined as having a nucleus, in which are found protons, with orbiting electrons (as described by famous quantum scientist Niels Bohr). For simplicity we will take the hydrogen atom as an example, since it only has one proton and one electron.

The way the electron stays in orbit of the proton is in this case not gravity but electric charge. The electron has a negative charge compared to the proton who has a positive charge, and as it is known opposite charges attract, which is how the electron is confined to the atom. Now if the electron managed to acquire energy and enough to obtain a positive charge, it would no longer be attracted to the proton and be free to wonder the universe. This way of acquiring energy can pe performed through a process called absorption, in which the electron absorbs a passing photon, particles of light, and gains energy.

The particle wavefunction is what explains the wave behaviour of fundamental particles, matter included. Probably the most famous of experiments in the domain is the double slit experiment, shown above. The experiment consists of shooting electrons, for example, at a double slitted wall towards a second one behind it that lights up at the spots where it gets hit.

Later on, Schrodinger introduces the now famous Wave equation to quantum mechanics, which models the wave behavior of particles of light for example. This may give the impression that the single particle is as if spread out into a wave, and not a particle anymore. In reality, Quantum physics later offered an idea to reconcile the wave vs. particle debate. This idea was wave-particle duality. To clarify, the wave is composed of peaks and troughs. Peaks, high points in the waves, actually indicate that the particle has a high probability of being where the peak is. Troughs, on the other hand, indicate that the particle has a low probability of being found there. Now, you might be thinking, why probability? Well the very novelty of quantum physics vs. traditional physics is that we can never be 100% certain about the position and speed of particles. In essence, particles are mysterious and uncertain. On the contrary, when it comes to regular macroscopic objects, like cars, buses, people, we can always determine position and speed. But not for microscopic particles. So this wave models this uncertain behavior. It tells us, thanks to a wave, where the particle is most likely to be or not to be. So, coming back to the example of light, photons (light particles) are definitely particles, but they move in such an uncertain way that their movement can be modeled by a wave of probability.

Emission and Absorption are key functions of the happenings of an atom. These concepts are basically how electrons obtain and release electric charge and how it effects the atom.

The first process shown is absorption showing an incoming photon (light) heading towards the atom. As the photon hits the electron it can be absorbed by it and the electron will "jump" to a higher energy level. The second is when, after some time, the electron releases this energy in the form of an ejected photon. One of the restriction of this process though is the quantification, or division, of energy that is translated visually in the energy levels. Energy levels represent the possible energies of an electron, but they are only so many because an electron can only have so many possible energy levels. This means that it can only find itself on one of the energy level rings, and not in between, subsequently showing that depending on the specific energy of the photon the electron may or may not acquire energy and jump levels. In this case, the photon goes right through.
